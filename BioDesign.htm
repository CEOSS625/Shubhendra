1.     Overview

In our research with Bio-inspired Desing, we are trying to develop a framework to understand what type of analogies lead to successful engineering design solutions using biology as inspiration.

In the current scenario, the study on design analogies is focused on overcoming cognitive challenges in bioinspired design and analogy, as discussed by Linsey, J.S., Viswanathan, V.K. (2014). [1] & Jiang, S., Hu, J. [2]. In recent years, even with the development of data-driven approaches [2], there has been a paucity of standardized benchmarking datasets, making it difficult to evaluate and compare the performance of different Designs by Analogy (DbA) tools and methods. Also, Deep learning models used in DbA often suffer from a lack of transparency, making it hard for users to understand why a particular analogy was retrieved or recommended. For these reasons, our research focuses only on overcoming these challenges. We are using the underlying work of Agyemang, M., Linsey, J., and Turner, C. J. [3] to develop an initial framework to compare or study what design analogies are used in Bio-Inspired Electromechanical systems.

 

We hypothesize that only certain types of analogies are showing up as successful. We believe there is a bias in selecting design analogies in Bio-inspired designs. We do not know whether other design analogies that were not considered were ignored due to current scenario limitations or if other analogy types do not work well for biology-inspired engineering.

 

2.     Motivation

 

Designers rely on their own memory and experience to retrieve analogies, which limits creativity, especially for novice designers. Human analogical reasoning is not always reliable or consistent and can lead to design fixation [4]. In the domain of design by analogy there is need of structured non cognitive and non-human-centered approach of using design by analogies. For this reason, a large database is required that can be used to evaluate what type of analogies are used in design. To create a structured database that will help future development of data driven DbA (Design by Analogies) we are to justify the relationship between what types of bio-inspired design analogies are used in Bio-Inspired Electromechanical systems. This will pave a future path for overcoming challenges discussed by Katherine Fu, 2014 [4]

  1. Mapping Difficulties: Designers find it hard to transfer biological strategies to engineering problems due to misaligned abstraction levels),
  2. Distance of analogy: Far-field analogies are often more creative but harder to retrieve and apply; defining optimal "analogical distance" is still an open question.
  3. Commonness: Familiar analogies can lead to fixation; too unfamiliar analogies may be incomprehensible

 

3.     Intellectual Merit

 

  1. What types of analogies are commonly used in bio-inspired designs?
  2. How do these analogies align with biological strategies documented in databases like AskNature.org?

 

Hypothesis: Biological Strategies exhibit structural and functional similarities with Electromechanical systems, which can be captured using critical chain analysis. For the successfully implemented bio-inspired design, we are trying to see if there is any bias in selecting a particular type of bio-inspired analogy. If yes, then why is there bias, and what possible reasons for this bias are involved? If not, then we know that the current progress in bio-inspired design is on a progressive path without any bias. The Critical factor here is to evaluate bias in design. As discussed earlier, it is one of the main challenges is using or studying design by analogy.

5.     Research Plan

 

Our research plan is to review the bio-inspired design available in literature. Our objective is to cover as many designs as possible and see what patterns and comparisons we observe regarding the type of analogies adopted. The framework we are using to study each design is shown below:

 

To overcome a scarcity of benchmark datasets for training and evaluating analogy in systems, we are using limited functional vocabulary, pruning rules established by Gill et al. (2016)[8] and Caldwell and Mocko (2008) [7] to make comparison easy across studies using critical chain analysis. Existing analogy databases (e.g., DANE, Idea-Inspire) are manually curated, small-scale, and lack scalability due to the time-intensive nature of functional modeling. To this challenge, we are using AI to automate the process of critical chain generation by using the 10-step algorithm with some validation steps. The 10-step algorithm process is added to address challenges related to AI, like some inaccuracies, biases, limitations in understanding context, and difficulty with long-form content.
