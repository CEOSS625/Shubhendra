Product Innovation for Social Impact at Club First

Overview

As a Research Associate at Club First, I designed and prototyped over 5 innovative products for real-world applications, working at the intersection of robotics, automation, and social impact. My most significant contribution was the development of the Instant Water Purification and Vending System (IWPVS)—engineered to provide safe drinking water in underserved rural areas of India. This product was developed in collaboration with the Naandi Foundation under Deutsche Bank’s CSR initiative. Beyond product design, I played multiple roles—including Design Engineer, Product Manager, Liaison Officer, and Sales Executive—to manage end-to-end development, client interaction, and funding proposals.

Time Study & Cycle Time Optimization

I conducted a detailed time study on Honda's engine assembly line, identifying inefficiencies during petrol-to-diesel jig changeovers. The analysis revealed a 38.5-second takt time loss per changeover. This led to the development of a Master Jig to eliminate downtime and improve production flow.

Ergonomic Hook Redesign for Safety

I redesigned the engine unloading hook using ergonomic principles and structural analysis. The new design incorporated load-bearing reinforcements to ensure safe and effortless handling, minimizing the risk of injury and enhancing operator comfort on the shop floor.

Master Jig Design Using CAD Tools

Using Autodesk Inventor, I designed a universal Master Jig compatible with both petrol and diesel engines. This eliminated the need for separate jigs, reduced manpower, and optimized assembly time and floor space. Prototyping validated alignment and prevented part fouling.

Visual Work Instructions for Hose Assembly

To support operator training, I developed a visual guide for hose pipe assembly. This improved process standardization, reduced error rates, and made the onboarding of new workers faster and more effective, aligning with Honda’s quality objectives.

Assembly Line Layout & Capacity Planning

I proposed a revised assembly engine layout using AutoCAD Mechanical to support new model integration and increased engine pallet capacity. This layout improved space utilization and readiness for future expansions in line with Honda’s Kaizen philosophy.

Project Insights

Problem Statement

The internship focused on identifying key inefficiencies in Honda’s engine assembly line, impacting takt time, safety, and production scalability.

  1. Separate jigs were used for petrol and diesel engine blocks, causing delays during model changeovers
  2. Each jig replacement added approximately 38.5 seconds of takt time to the assembly process.
  3. The process required additional manpower for every changeover, increasing labor cost and inefficiency.
  4. Manual jig handling created a safety hazard, with a risk of slipping or dropping heavy components.
  5. No standardized method existed for hose pipe assembly, leading to inconsistency and training challenges.
  6. The existing engine unloading hook lacked ergonomic design, causing operator fatigue and inefficiencies.
  7. The current assembly engine layout was not optimized for future model expansion, limiting scalability.

Conclusion

Through a detailed time study, we discovered that frequent jig changeovers between petrol and diesel engine blocks were adding 38.5 seconds of takt time per engine. Takt time is the maximum allowable time to produce a product to meet customer demand — and reducing it is critical for improving efficiency. To address this issue, I designed a Master Jigthat accommodates both engine types on a single fixture, eliminating manual jig swaps. This solution streamlined the assembly process, reduced labor requirements, improved operator safety, and supported Honda’s Kaizen-driven approach to continuous improvement.
